Why High Impedance (Hi-Z) Exists: A Concept Every Embedded Engineer Should Understand

Whenever we start learning embedded systems, one of the very first things we do is configure a GPIO pin either as an input or an output. We write something like pinMode(INPUT) in Arduino, configure the MODER register in STM32, or set the TRIS register in PIC. It feels like just another configuration step, and most of us move on without thinking about what actually changes inside the microcontroller.

But something very interesting happens internally when we configure a pin as an input.

To understand it, let's first think about what an output pin really does.

Imagine you connect an LED to a GPIO pin. When your program writes a logic HIGH, the microcontroller actively applies a voltage to that pin. When it writes a logic LOW, it actively connects the pin to ground. In other words, the microcontroller is not simply storing a value in memory — it is electrically driving the wire.

You can think of the GPIO pin as a person holding a rope. If that person pulls the rope upward, the rope moves up. If they pull it downward, the rope moves down. The person is in complete control of the rope's movement.

Now let's replace that LED with a push button.

Our goal is no longer to control the voltage. Instead, we want the button to decide whether the pin is HIGH or LOW, while the microcontroller simply reads the result.

At first glance, this seems straightforward. But here's an important question.

What happens if the microcontroller still tries to drive the pin HIGH while the button connects that same pin directly to ground?

Now two different sources are trying to control the exact same wire.

The microcontroller says,

"This wire must be 5V."

The button says,

"No, this wire is connected to 0V."

Both are correct from their own perspective, but electrically they are fighting each other. Instead of exchanging information, current now flows directly from the power supply to ground through the output driver.

This situation is called Bus Contention.

Bus contention occurs whenever two or more devices attempt to drive the same signal line simultaneously, especially when they try to drive opposite logic levels. One device may be forcing the line HIGH while another forces it LOW. Since neither device is willing to let go, a large amount of current can flow between them.

Depending on the circuit, this may simply produce incorrect logic levels, increase power consumption, or in the worst case, permanently damage the output drivers.

Clearly, we need a way to prevent this.

This is exactly why High Impedance, often written as Hi-Z, exists.

When a GPIO pin is configured as an input, the microcontroller does something very important internally. It disconnects its output driver from the pin. The pin is no longer connected to the internal circuitry responsible for driving HIGH or LOW.

In other words, the microcontroller lets go of the rope.

It doesn't pull the wire HIGH.

It doesn't pull the wire LOW.

It simply watches.

This electrical state is called High Impedance.

The word "impedance" refers to how much a circuit resists current flow. In the Hi-Z state, the pin presents an extremely high impedance to the outside world, meaning almost no current flows into or out of the microcontroller through that pin. From the perspective of the external circuit, it's almost as if the microcontroller isn't connected at all.

This allows another device — a button, a sensor, another microcontroller, or even an entire communication bus — to safely determine the voltage on that line without interference.

A simple way to think about it is this:

An output pin is like speaking into a conversation.

An input pin (Hi-Z) is like quietly listening.

If everyone speaks at once, communication fails.

If only one person speaks while everyone else listens, communication works perfectly.

This idea extends far beyond GPIO inputs.

Consider an SPI bus with multiple slave devices. All of the slaves share the same MISO wire, but only one slave is allowed to transmit data at any given moment. The selected slave actively drives the line, while every other slave places its MISO pin into the High Impedance state. If two slaves attempted to drive MISO simultaneously, their outputs could conflict, leading to bus contention.

The same principle appears in I²C communication, although it works a little differently. Devices on an I²C bus never actively drive the line HIGH. They either pull it LOW or release it completely. Releasing the line means entering the Hi-Z state, allowing the pull-up resistor to bring the line HIGH. This clever design allows many devices to safely share the same communication wires.

Even inside computers, processors, RAM, Flash memory, and external peripherals share common data buses. At any instant, only one device is allowed to drive the bus while every other device remains in High Impedance. Without this mechanism, modern digital systems simply wouldn't function reliably.

So the next time you configure a GPIO pin as an input, don't just think,

"I'm making this pin an input."

Think about what is actually happening electrically.

You are telling the microcontroller,

"Stop driving this wire. Disconnect your output stage. Let the external world control the voltage, and simply observe what happens."

That single concept — letting go of the wire — is what High Impedance really means.

And once you understand High Impedance, you'll realize it isn't just a GPIO feature. It's one of the fundamental ideas that makes shared communication buses, memory systems, and modern digital electronics possible.
